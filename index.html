Waves of Feminism

  • First Wave: 19th–early 20th century — Voting rights
  • Second Wave: 1960s–1980s — Workplace, family, reproductive rights
  • Third Wave: 1990s–2000s — Individualism, diversity, intersectionality
  • Fourth Wave: 2010s–present — Online activism, body positivity, inclusion

Common Myths About Feminism

No. Feminism advocates for equality for all genders.
Feminism benefits everyone by promoting fairness and challenging harmful gender norms.
No. Feminism advocates for gender equality — not female supremacy. True feminism includes the liberation of all genders from harmful roles and stereotypes.
Feminism challenges toxic masculinity, emotional suppression, and rigid gender roles — all of which harm men too. Equality is liberating for everyone.
Feminists are rightfully angry at injustice — not men as individuals. That stereotype exists to discredit people speaking out about inequality.
Despite progress, gender pay gaps, underrepresentation, violence against women, and societal double standards still persist globally. Feminism is still very necessary.
Feminism is about choice. You can wear makeup, love fashion, and still demand equal rights. Feminine expression and feminism are not mutually exclusive.

My Story

As Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie once said, women are born inherently guilty. My whole life I was accused of things I never did or even thought of doing — while my brother went unquestioned, free to do as he pleased.

I was taught that women should get married, that not doing so meant failure. But men? They were just “taking their time.” That stuck with me.

I grew up being told Eve was made for Adam — that women were made for men. And when a woman is good, the praise often isn’t “good for you,” but “good for you for being good for your future husband.” Our value is still framed around someone else.

Even in our titles, the inequality is etched in: men are Mr. — always. Women are Miss, Ms., or Mrs., marked by whether or not they’ve been claimed.

Even as a single woman, other people’s relationships become our problem. We’re expected to behave a certain way because “he has a girlfriend” or “don’t lead him on.” Because boys will be boys — and it’s our job to manage them.

Even if a woman behaves perfectly — kind, quiet, modest, smart, careful — she can still be condemned. And even if a man is the worst man in the world, he will not be. If a woman is perfect, she won’t be praised. But if a man shows even a little bit of decency, the world applauds him.

There's a statistic I once heard: the happiest people are married men and unmarried women. Because women carry the emotional labor, the caregiving, the burden.

A woman can lose her job over something small, something “inappropriate” or “unbecoming.” But Donald Trump, accused by multiple women of sexual assault, became President. What message does that send?

The Barbie movie wasn’t a fantasy. It was a saddening documentary. The Barbies represented men; the Kens, women. And in the end, when the Barbies said, “Let’s just be a little nicer to the Kens,” it wasn’t a happy ending — just an honest one. It reminded me that women’s equality isn’t a matter of effort, but of permission. And that permission is too rarely granted.

Global Feminist Icons

Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie

Nigerian author and speaker known for her TED talk "We Should All Be Feminists" — a modern feminist manifesto.

Gloria Steinem

American journalist and leader of the second-wave feminist movement. Co-founder of Ms. magazine.

Malala Yousafzai

Pakistani education activist and Nobel Peace Prize winner who fought for girls' right to education after surviving a Taliban attack.

Angela Davis

American political activist and scholar who connects feminism to race, class, and the prison industrial complex.

Frida Kahlo

Mexican artist whose art and identity challenged gender norms and celebrated female strength, pain, and power.

Sojourner Truth

Former slave and abolitionist who famously asked, "Ain’t I a Woman?" — a rallying cry for intersectional feminism in the 19th century.

Susan B. Anthony

American suffragist who helped secure women’s right to vote in the U.S., though her movement often excluded Black women.

Emmeline Pankhurst

British suffragette who led radical protests for women's voting rights in the early 20th century.

Ruth Bader Ginsburg

U.S. Supreme Court Justice who fought legal battles for gender equality and women's rights for decades.
