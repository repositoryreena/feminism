Waves of Feminism

  • First Wave: 19th–early 20th century — Voting rights
  • Second Wave: 1960s–1980s — Workplace, family, reproductive rights
  • Third Wave: 1990s–2000s — Individualism, diversity, intersectionality
  • Fourth Wave: 2010s–present — Online activism, body positivity, inclusion

Common Myths About Feminism

No. Feminism advocates for equality for all genders.
Feminism benefits everyone by promoting fairness and challenging harmful gender norms.
No. Feminism advocates for gender equality — not female supremacy. True feminism includes the liberation of all genders from harmful roles and stereotypes.
Feminism challenges toxic masculinity, emotional suppression, and rigid gender roles — all of which harm men too. Equality is liberating for everyone.

My Story

As Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie once said, women have often been made to feel responsible for things beyond their control. But awareness brings the power to shift that narrative.

Growing up, I noticed how expectations differed for girls and boys. That realization stayed with me — and inspired me to question, learn, and grow.

I was taught that marriage was essential for women, while men were seen as simply “taking their time.” It opened my eyes to how deeply cultural norms shape our self-worth — and the importance of defining our own paths.

I heard stories of how women were "made for men," yet I now believe we’re each made for purpose — for meaning, not for someone else’s validation. We deserve to be celebrated for who we are, not who we serve.

Language matters. Even in our titles, small differences reflect big assumptions. Recognizing that is the first step toward building more equal ground.

Sometimes, women are expected to manage others' emotions and behaviors, especially in relationships. But we each deserve to exist freely, without being held responsible for someone else’s choices.

A woman can do everything right and still face challenges — not because she is flawed, but because the systems around her are still evolving. And still, women continue to lead, grow, and succeed with grace.

I once heard this: the happiest people are married men and unmarried women. Perhaps that’s because women are learning to put their own well-being first — and that’s a powerful thing.

Society is full of contradictions. But calling them out isn’t about blame — it’s about building clarity, understanding, and fairness for everyone.

For me, faith provides balance. At its best, religion offers shared standards and mutual respect, while society often still wrestles with outdated double standards. But together, we can shape a more just world.

When I was dating a programmer, people said I was lucky. But real luck was realizing my own potential — and becoming a programmer myself. That choice changed my life.

I believe every woman — whether a first lady, a star, a mother, a coder, or all of the above — brings something valuable. Every story matters. Every life has dignity. No woman’s worth is defined by who she’s with.

I love men, and I admire women — all women. I believe in choice, freedom, and mutual respect. Whether someone chooses a career, a family, or both, that decision deserves honor, not judgment.

When I watched the Barbie movie, I didn’t see a fantasy. I saw a reflection — a reminder that equality isn't about asking for a seat at the table. It’s about knowing we’ve always deserved one.

Global Feminist Icons

Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie

Nigerian author and speaker known for her TED talk "We Should All Be Feminists" — a modern feminist manifesto.

Gloria Steinem

American journalist and leader of the second-wave feminist movement. Co-founder of Ms. magazine.

Malala Yousafzai

Pakistani education activist and Nobel Peace Prize winner who fought for girls' right to education after surviving a Taliban attack.

Angela Davis

American political activist and scholar who connects feminism to race, class, and the prison industrial complex.

Frida Kahlo

Mexican artist whose art and identity challenged gender norms and celebrated female strength, pain, and power.

Sojourner Truth

Former slave and abolitionist who famously asked, "Ain’t I a Woman?" — a rallying cry for intersectional feminism in the 19th century.

Susan B. Anthony

American suffragist who helped secure women’s right to vote in the U.S., though her movement often excluded Black women.

Emmeline Pankhurst

British suffragette who led radical protests for women's voting rights in the early 20th century.

Ruth Bader Ginsburg

U.S. Supreme Court Justice who fought legal battles for gender equality and women's rights for decades.
